# Chapter 3: German Numbers (Zahlen)

Learning numbers in German is highly logical, though it has one famous quirk: for numbers 21 to 99, Germans say the units first, followed by the tens (e.g., "one-and-twenty" instead of "twenty-one").

---

These are the foundational numbers. Memorize these thoroughly as they form the building blocks for all larger numbers.

## Numbers 21–99: The Reverse Rule

For numbers 21 through 99, German combines the unit digit and the tens digit using the word **und** (and). 
* **Formula**: `[Unit] + und + [Tens]`
* *Note*: When **eins** (1) is used in a combined number, it loses its "s" and becomes **ein**.

```
21 -> einundzwanzig (one-and-twenty) -> "EYN-oont-tsvan-tsikh"
35 -> fünfunddreißig (five-and-thirty) -> "FWENF-oont-dry-sikh"
67 -> siebenundsechzig (seven-and-sixty) -> "ZEE-ben-oont-zekh-tsikh"
```

### The Tens Digits
Notice that most tens digits end in **-zig**, except for thirty which ends in **-ßig**.

* **30**: dreißig (*DRY-sikh*)
* **40**: vierzig (*FEER-tsikh*)
* **50**: fünfzig (*FWENF-tsikh*)
* **60**: sechzig (*ZEKH-tsikh*) — *loses the 's' from sechs*
* **70**: siebzig (*ZEEP-tsikh*) — *loses the 'en' from sieben*
* **80**: achtzig (*AKHT-tsikh*)
* **90**: neunzig (*NOYN-tsikh*)

## Hundreds, Thousands, and Beyond

German builds large numbers by simply stacking the words together into a single, massive word.

* **100**: (ein)hundert (*HOON-dert*)
* **101**: hundertundeins (*HOON-dert-oont-eynts*) or simply *hunderteins*
* **200**: zweihundert (*TSVY-hoon-dert*)
* **1,000**: (ein)tausend (*TOW-zent*)
* **5,432**: fünftausendvierhundertzweiunddreißig (*FWENF-tow-zent-FEER-hoon-dert-tsvy-oont-DRY-sikh*)

### Millions and Billions
Unlike hundreds and thousands, millions and billions are treated as separate feminine nouns and change in the plural.

* **1 Million (1,000,000)**: eine Million (*EY-nuh mil-YOHN*)
* **2 Millions (2,000,000)**: zwei Millionen (*TSVY mil-YOH-nen*)
* **1 Billion (1,000,000,000)**: eine Milliarde (*EY-nuh mil-YAR-duh*)
* **2 Billions (2,000,000,000)**: zwei Milliarden (*TSVY mil-YAR-den*)

## Ordinal Numbers

Ordinal numbers (first, second, third, etc.) are used for dates, rankings, and lists. In German, they are treated as adjectives and require adjective endings (usually **-te** or **-ten**).

* **1st**: erste (*EHR-stuh*) — *irregular*
* **2nd**: zweite (*TSVY-tuh*)
* **3rd**: dritte (*DRIT-tuh*) — *irregular*
* **4th**: vierte (*FEER-tuh*)
* **7th**: siebte (*ZEEP-tuh*) — *irregular*
* **8th**: achte (*AKH-tuh*)
* **19th**: neunzehnte (*NOYN-tsayn-tuh*)
* **20th**: zwanzigste (*TSVAN-tsikh-stuh*) — *note the 's' before 'te' from 20 onwards*
* **21st**: einundzwanzigste (*EYN-oont-tsvan-tsikh-stuh*)

> [!IMPORTANT]
> When writing ordinal numbers in German using digits, a **period** is placed after the number.
> * **Der 1. Mai** = Der erste Mai (The 1st of May)
> * **Er wohnt im 3. Stock** = Er wohnt im dritten Stock (He lives on the 3rd floor)

## Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages

### Fractions
Fractions are formed by adding **-tel** (for numbers under 19) or **-stel** (for numbers 20 and above) to the cardinal number.
* **1/3**: ein Drittel (*EYN DRIT-tel*)
* **1/4**: ein Viertel (*EYN FEER-tel*)
* **1/2**: halb (*hahlp*) as an adjective, or *die Hälfte* (the half) as a noun.

### Decimals
German uses a **comma** (Komma) instead of a decimal point.
* **1.5** (English) is written as **1,5** (German) -> *eins Komma fünf*
* **0.75** (English) is written as **0,75** (German) -> *null Komma sieben fünf*

### Percentages
* **%** is written and spoken as **Prozent** (*pro-TSENT*).
* **50%**: fünfzig Prozent (*FWENF-tsikh pro-TSENT*)

---

## Practical Applications

### 1. Money (Geld)
German uses the Euro (€). Prices are spoken by stating the Euros first, then the cents.
* **12,50 €**: zwölf Euro fünfzig (*TSVUELF OY-ro FWENF-tsikh*)

### 2. Telephone Numbers
Phone numbers are usually spoken as individual digits, sometimes grouped in pairs.
* **0172 34567**: *null eins sieben zwei, drei vier fünf sechs sieben*

### 3. Years (Jahreszahlen)
* For years before 2000, express them as hundreds:
  * **1995**: neunzehnhundertfünfundneunzig (*NOYN-tsayn-hoon-dert-fwenf-oont-noyn-tsikh*)
* For years 2000 and later, express them as cardinal numbers:
  * **2026**: zweitausendsechsundzwanzig (*TSVY-tow-zent-zekhs-oont-tsvan-tsikh*)
